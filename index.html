warning Problem Overview
warning Context

Food insecurity means not having reliable access to enough food. It affects millions of Americans and causes health, education, and financial issues. Knowing where food insecurity will happen can help stop it before it gets worse.

help Need

Most research looks at food insecurity after it happens, not before. We want to use data to predict future food insecurity at the county level. This helps fill a gap in current research and makes it more useful for real-world help.

build Task

We collected data on food insecurity and related factors like poverty and unemployment. We used machine learning to try and predict future food insecurity. We interviewed a professor who specializes in social work, as well as local food bank workers to get real-world insight on this crisis.

dashboard Overview

First, we will review and discuss past research and background information on food insecurity as a whole. Then, we will explain our data, methods, and models that we used for this project. Next, we will show our results and explain what they mean. Finally, we will discuss what this could mean for future policy and research in regards to food insecurity.

data_explorer Data Sources
  • Used Food Access Research Atlas by USDA Economic Research Service
  • Sources: 2010 Census, ACS (2014–2018), SNAP‑authorized store data (STARS & TDLinx)
  • Grid‑based spatial allocation maps food access at census‑tract level
  • Computes supermarket distances (½ mile urban, 10–20 miles rural) and vehicle access metrics
  • Limitations: uses older census/ACS estimates, straight‑line distances, excludes informal food sources
map Map the Meal Gap & Preprocessing
  • Used Feeding America’s Map the Meal Gap dataset covering 3,144 counties
  • Data from CPS, ACS, BLS, and NielsenIQ under USDA Thrifty Food Plan guidelines
  • Tracks race/ethnicity, income, SNAP eligibility, child insecurity, and meal cost
  • Cleaned & merged: standardized county names; limited years to 2014–2022
  • Reshaped datasets: extracted “Year” column and aligned by location
analytics Modeling Techniques
  • Logistic regression to predict high food insecurity (FI_High)
  • Predictors: poverty, unemployment, income level, SNAP thresholds, race, child insecurity
  • Converted NaNs to “missing”, standardized types, removed no‑variation columns
  • Evaluation via prediction tables and confusion matrix
  • Final model achieved 93% accuracy
insights Key Findings
trending_up Rising Costs

Average cost per meal increased by over 30% from 2019 to 2022, making food less affordable for vulnerable populations.

child_care Child Impact

Child food insecurity rates exceed 30% in many counties, with the majority of food insecure children living in households below 185% of the federal poverty line.

diversity_3 Racial Disparities

Food insecurity rates among Black and Hispanic populations are consistently higher than among white populations across nearly all counties studied.

coronavirus Pandemic Effect

While some areas saw temporary improvements in 2021, most counties experienced significant increases in food insecurity by 2022, exceeding pre-pandemic levels.

developer_board Deep Learning Analysis

The deep learning approach utilized fastai's tabular model framework, preprocessing the dataset using TabularPandas with transformations such as Categorify, FillMissing, and Normalize. A logistic regression model was also tested using statsmodels' Logit to predict high food insecurity (FI_High), incorporating predictors like racial and ethnic group insecurity rates, SNAP thresholds, and child food insecurity. Despite using MLE, the logistic model underperformed. The deep learning model, trained using a 4-layer neural network (400-200-100) with a learning rate finder between 0.03 and 0.01 and a batch size of 256, achieved up to 93% accuracy.

record_voice_over Monica Adams: Voices from the Front Lines

Food Insecurity Researcher, Broome County Food Council

info Causes and Definitions

  • Low wages and transportation barriers
    “Some of the most significant causes are low wages, low income, there's not a lot of jobs here that pay higher salaries, so obviously income is a factor [and] employment challenges go along with that: limited transportation, availability of grocery stores like the distance to get to them.”
  • Moving from 'food desert' to 'food apartheid'
    “If you think about a desert, this is more like an ecosystem and food deserts just aren't these naturally occurring things that happened. These things are human‑made, our policies and how we make decisions... Food apartheid is more reflective of what's happening. It's an oppressive system that is restricting access to food because of bureaucracy... [and] how we design cities.”

help Local Solutions and Challenges

  • COVID collaboration: Broome County Food Council
    “...COVID hit so hard, so fast, and really changed the dynamic of the food assistance programs. It... forced people to start talking to each other, which was a good thing because out of this came the Broome County Food Council.”
  • Greater Good Grocery mobile market
    “...There's a grocery store here called the Greater Good Grocery Store. It's a nonprofit brick and mortar store that started a mobile food market... It's a school bus that's been turned into a little miniature grocery store that drives around the county to these areas where we have food apartheid and it sells food, so it has [fresh produce, refrigerated, and frozen items] and that's had an immediate impact because people who did not have access to grocery stores, now I have this bus that pulls up once a week [where] they can jump on and get food and the food is affordable.”

group Populations and Policy Impacts

  • Affected populations
    “Most obviously people with low incomes, but within that population, women and children have the highest rates of food insecurity in the country and in the area... Women than men to live in poverty and food insecurity and poverty are connected. Anybody who has the minority identity, racial, ethnic minorities, LGBTQ community, people with disabilities, they have higher rates of food insecurity compared to other people with low income... Another population would be the elderly and that's a combination of income and issues like mobility access because it's different for us even if we live on a bus line to just jump on the bus and run to the store.”
  • Trade‑offs between food and other expenses
    “ It's this constant. Struggle of having to prioritize: Do I eat? Do I sit in the dark? But If I choose to buy the food, but the electricity gets cut off. I can't cook the food. I can't store the food in my refrigerator because I have no power. So some bills are always going to trump food because it just doesn't make sense to buy food if you can't cook it or store it. Similarly, if I don't pay my rent and I get evicted, where am I am [going to] keep this food. So yes, absolutely, housing costs, utility bills, these things impact food insecurity. Health and food insecurity are so intertwined.”
summarize Conclusions & Evidence Summary
developer_board Model Results

We compared a logistic regression model to a deep neural network. The logistic regression (with predictors like poverty, unemployment, race, SNAP eligibility, and child insecurity) underperformed, while the deep learning model (4-layer neural network, trained with fastai on tabular data) reached about 93% accuracy. Important features driving the model were race/ethnicity (food insecurity rates by group), household income/SNAP thresholds, and child food insecurity levels.

query_stats Chart Insights

State-level charts reveal stark disparities: Black and Hispanic communities face much higher food insecurity rates than white communities. Children’s food insecurity is notably severe (often above 30%). Food costs have surged (~30% increase in average meal price from 2019 to 2022). Time-trend analysis shows that by 2022 most counties had food insecurity levels exceeding pre-pandemic.

forum Interview Reflections

Monica Adams highlights systemic barriers: she cites persistent low wages and neighborhood “food deserts” (or food apartheid) due to historical planning. Local solutions include the Broome County Food Council’s food access plan and a nonprofit grocery’s mobile market (a bus delivering affordable fresh food to underserved areas). Adams also recounts families’ difficult trade-offs like choosing between buying food or paying the electric bill, or between healthy food and necessary medicine.

error_outline Data Limitations

Our analysis relies on public data with known gaps. Food access data uses older census/ACS estimates (e.g. 2010 Census, 2014–2018 ACS) and simple distance metrics. We work at the county level for the ML model, which can mask within-county variation, and we do not capture informal food sources (like community pantries). These limitations mean some recent changes or local resources may be underrepresented in our findings.

gavel Policy Implications

Policies should target the identified needs: for example, expanding SNAP benefits and child nutrition programs helps struggling families. Efforts must address root causes like housing affordability and healthcare costs to improve food security. Supporting local initiatives (food banks, mobile markets) and focusing resources on high-risk counties will ensure aid reaches the communities most in need.
